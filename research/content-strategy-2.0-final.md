## Executive Summary

STAGE is shifting from a **culture-first** to a **format-first** content strategy. The core realization: great stories are universal — culture is what makes them personal and distributable across markets. Budget will now be allocated by **format** (not by regional market), with a unified **STAGE slate** that gets adopted into each culture. Every piece of content must explicitly solve for either **acquisition** or **retention** (or both). Non-fiction is the biggest untapped opportunity. Three new TAM segments — kids, devotional, music — should be explored in an AI-native production model.

---

## 1. The Strategic Shift

### Before (Culture-First)
- Content strategy organized by regional market (Haryanvi slate, Rajasthani slate, etc.)
- Separate budgets per culture
- Every culture gets everything
- "Make 12 films in every market"

### Now (Format-First)
- One **STAGE slate** adopted across all cultures
- Budget allocated by **format** (long series, binge, film, micro drama, non-fiction)
- Stories can originate from any culture
- Culture is the **distribution superpower**, not the creative starting point
- "Make 2 tentpole IPs instead of 10 mediocre films"

### What Changed
> *"Earlier, we were making four frameworks. Now, I will make one framework, but it will be very strong."* — HMT

- The central approach was "everyone should get everything" — that worked to get STAGE here, but now the team is in a better position to make big, confident decisions
- Budget allocation shifts from per-market to per-format
- Mindset shift from "₹50 lakh per content" to "invest heavily in fewer, bigger bets"

## 4. Content Architecture: Four Layers

Each layer has a distinct purpose in the user's life. The same user moves across layers depending on their moment.

### Layer 1 — Habit Engine (60% of Investment)

**Purpose:** Build daily/weekly viewing habits. Without this, nothing else matters.

**Format:** Long series, episodic content

**Why long series work:**
- **Appointment viewing** — user knows "my show drops at 7 PM"
- **Character attachment** — after 10 episodes, characters feel like family
- **Low decision cost** — user doesn't choose, they just continue
- **Cliffhanger mechanism** — each episode ends with a reason to return
- **Community shared pace** — people discuss together, creating social glue

**Character-First Mandate:**
Every long series pitch must start with:
1. Who is the protagonist?
2. What's their want?
3. Why will the audience root for them?
4. What world can sustain long storytelling?
5. What's the cliffhanger archetype?

### Layer 2 — Variety Platter (20% of Investment)

**Purpose:** Platform perception — "there's always something new." Prevents STAGE from being seen as a "daily soap app."

**Formats:** Non-fiction, diverse genres, mixed content

**Non-Fiction — The Big Unlock:**
- In the AI world, real human stories become premium
- Production cost = 30-50% of scripted content
- Culturally specific without dubbing issues
- Proven retention globally (Netflix's most-watched are often non-fiction)
- Cross-culture IPs possible (e.g., food competitions between cultures, teaching one culture's language to another)

> *"No one has done much in non-fiction yet in the market we serve. There is a lot of potential to create IPs that cut across all cultures."* — HMT

**Non-Fiction Sub-Formats (to be categorized):**
- Reality shows (live or produced)
- True crime
- Cultural competition formats
- Episodic non-fiction (e.g., Saptrang)
- Live events

### Layer 3 — FOMO Engine

**Purpose:** Social buzz, organic acquisition. The most powerful growth lever.

**Psychology:** When 3 friends are watching something and the 4th is not — the 4th subscribes. No ad can replicate this.

> *"FOMO is the most powerful organic acquisition tool. This is STAGE's biggest gap."* — HMT

**How FOMO is created:**
- High completion rate content that becomes conversation
- Weekly appointment viewing that creates shared cultural moments
- Content so buzzy that dormant users reactivate

### Layer 4 — Acquisition Magnet (Tentpole Events)

**Purpose:** Drive awareness and trials. NOT retention.

**Key insight:** Feature films don't drive retention. They drive awareness and trials. Use them as **marketing events**, not content strategy.

**Strategy:**
- Don't make 12 films → make 2-3 **massive tentpole IPs** per year
- Pull 3 months of marketing budget into 1 massive production
- Always pair tentpole releases with long series recommendations (convert trial users into habitual viewers)
- Start LED special music events monthly

**Tentpole Prediction Framework (to be built):**
HMT believes tentpole success is predictable before release:
> *"It's not like you know it only when it's made. If 10 things are in a content, then that content becomes a tentpole. We can build that frame."*

Example: Naate — HMT was personally sure it was tentpole and convinced the team. The framework of signals exists; it needs to be codified.

## 5. User Segmentation by Behavior

| Segment | Behavior | Need | Content Strategy |
|---------|----------|------|-----------------|
| **Hooked** | Follow 2-3 long series, open 4-5x/week | Continuity | Never let their show end without a replacement queued |
| **Browsers** | Open 1-2x/week, watch what looks interesting | Variety | Film, non-fiction, micro drama. Convert by getting them to start a long series |
| **Dormant** | Subscribed but barely open | Reason to return | Tentpole film, buzzworthy release, new season |
| **New** | Just subscribed, exploring | Immediate value | 3+ things in first 7 days → 60% retention target |

### Critical Scheduling Rules
- **Most dangerous moment:** When a long series ends
- **Never end two long series in the same month**
- **New series starts 2 weeks before existing one ends**
- **Last episode of ending series promotes the next series**
- **Push notification within 24 hours** after a show ends → recommend next

**Example lesson learned:** After Mahapunar Janam, brought Videshi Bahu. Ideally should have started hooks 2-3 episodes before the first series ended, to maintain momentum.

## 6. Thinking in Mood, Not Just Format

> *"We must serve every mood. The same user wants comedy, crime, long series, micro drama. User's psyche depends on mood."*

Content should be balanced across genres (crime, thriller, comedy, drama, romance) because users don't come in one mode. The release calendar should ensure **every month has 6 slots filled** with a genre balance.

**Ideally:** Every day has a reason to open STAGE — something new releasing (a new episode, a new title, a new format).

## 7. The Culture Debate: Resolved

### The Tension
HMT's presentation framed culture as a "distribution wrapper." Renu pushed back strongly: culture IS the content — characters, conflicts, stories are born from culture. Removing culture removes the soul.

### Multiple Perspectives Aired

**HMT's framing:**
- Culture and story are **equal** but **separable**
- The core of filmmaking (character design, plot, production value) is the **necessary condition** for content to work
- Cultural context alone is **not sufficient** — a culturally authentic but poorly made film won't work
- Culture enables distribution: Sabri (Rajasthani) works in Bhojpuri when dubbed because the core story is universal; adding cultural context expands the TAM

**Renu's position:**
- Culture gives birth to stories and conflict — you can't separate them
- "If I remove culture from Naate or Samri, people have universal emotions. But the uniqueness, the offbeat quality — that IS culture"
- Culture is like the moat around Mehrangarh fort — it's what makes STAGE defensible
- Danger of "universalizing" stories: forced character mixing dilutes authenticity

**Nishita's research perspective:**
- Field research in Patna showed different reasons for watching: Jhola Shah (cultural identity — "our village, our actor, our story") vs Sabri (intrigue about a different culture)
- "Urge would be to not simplify just because you want to come to a strategy"
- Culture creates emotional connection AND gives rise to conflict in stories — it operates at multiple layers

**Parveen's observation:**
- Culture also acts as a decision-maker in the user's mental model: "I want to watch some Haryanvi today" — that's a distribution lever
- Example: IPL commentary in Haryanvi (calling umpire "Tau") gets 5 lakh shares per reel — same content, cultural wrapper transforms distribution

### Resolution
The word "wrapper" is problematic and will be **reframed**. The agreed position:

1. **Culture and story are equal and intertwined** — stories are born from cultural contexts
2. **Authenticity is non-negotiable** — if shot in Rajasthan, it must be authentic Rajasthan
3. **The core of filmmaking is the necessary condition** — good characters, plot, production value make content work regardless of cultural context
4. **Culture is the distribution superpower** — it personalizes content for each market and expands TAM through dubbing/productization
5. **Not all stories need to be "universal"** — some are deeply rooted in one culture and that's their strength; others have universal emotions that travel
6. **Forced mixing doesn't work** — don't artificially insert characters from other cultures. If it comes organically in the story, great. If not, don't force it.

> *"Even today, when a story is born, that story will be told with the same level of authenticity. Otherwise, that story will neither be relevant for that place nor for anyone else."* — HMT

**HMT's learning from the debate:**
> *"I think she's saying right. Even I came to her and told her two or three times to bring a Haryana user, bring a character from Rajasthan. I think that is not the right way to think about the story."*

### The Binge Debate

Nishita questioned whether "binge" is a format or merely a release pattern. Extended discussion:

**HMT's position:** Binge is a **user mental model**, not just release timing.
- Binge gives the user **choice** — watch all at once, one per day, or spread across a week
- When a platform controls that choice (weekly release), user behavior changes
- Videshi Bahu 1 succeeded as a binge drop; VB2 struggles as weekly → same IP, different format, different results
- Marketing changes, intent changes, mental model changes with the format

**Nishita's pushback:** Every good content should have binge-worthy storytelling regardless of format. Is the distinction meaningful?

**Resolution:** Binge is a valid format (not just a release pattern) because it shapes user mental model, marketing strategy, and commitment psychology. However, **how to release** is a separate optimization problem within the format.

**Key data point:** Users who commit to 6 episodes think differently from users committing to 50. Shorter commitment = lower friction = broader appeal.

**Action:** Historical analysis needed — compare watch patterns across all binge series vs all long series to validate with data.

## 10. Content as Product: The Productization of Culture

The concept of "productizing culture" emerged as a key strategic idea:

- One great story → adapted with cultural authenticity into every regional market
- Beyond dubbing: hair, clothes, setting, dialect, local references all change
- Core emotion and story integrity remain unchanged
- AI-enabled localization can accelerate this

**Examples discussed:**
- Sabri (Rajasthani) → dubbed into Bhojpuri → Patna became highest viewership market
- IPL commentary dubbed in Haryanvi → 5 lakh shares per reel (same content, cultural layer transforms distribution)
- Kantara → smart dubbing (hero gets Mumbai accent, maintains South authenticity)

**What doesn't work:** Forcing characters from other cultures into a story for TAM expansion. If it doesn't come organically, don't force it.

## 11. Distribution Thinking

**Key insight:** Distribution hacks should be configured **during story development**, not post-production.

**Distribution levers embedded in content:**
- Offbeat/unique premise (intrinsic virality)
- Star cast (awareness)
- Influencer attachment (reach)
- Controversial elements (earned media — "negative PR is good PR")
- Cultural specificity (community ownership)
- Production value (premium perception)

**Market nuances matter:**
- Different markets have different consumption behaviors (Gujarati ≠ Bhojpuri ≠ Haryanvi)
- Best-performing films were canceled for ₹1 each in some markets (Bhuj, Patna) — distribution and pricing strategy must match market understanding
- Trial model vs direct subscription is a business call that varies by content and market maturity

**Parveen's distribution question:** "We haven't understood our distribution strengths or challenges well enough. If we have case studies of Sabri, Jhola Shah, Punar Janam being liked — what distribution understanding have we developed?"
