### 6.1 Adbhut India Presentation (May 2024)

**Title:** How STAGE OTT is Using AI to Break Language Barriers | Harsh, Co-founder of STAGE | Adbhut India  
**URL:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=xPB0cxeQFp0  
**Speaker:** HMT (solo presentation)  
**Channel:** STAGE (565K subs)

---

Hello everyone, thank you people plus AI for having us here and a very good evening to all of you.

[Opens with an advertisement example, technical difficulties]

So by and large — and to be fair this could happen to any of us on a hot summer afternoon. Now here's the thing, you know, it's safe to say that even in this hall all of us, despite being proficient in a certain language, we are carrying an inherent identity of another. It is the identity that speaks to your core, to your emotional core, and that is generally associated to your native tongue.

So as much as all of us are super proficient in English or Hindi, but there is something — there's something within us that helps us feel, that helps us think, that is our window to the world outside and also the window for the world towards us.

And that is precisely the insight that we have used to build STAGE. **A language is not just about access — it's about identity.**

So what is STAGE? STAGE is an entertainment platform for India's regional cultures.

And it turns out that when we reach our users in the lingo, in the native that they understand and they truly connect with, they shower us with a lot of love. When we go and tell them that hey, we are here to validate, reinforce and celebrate your identity, they are inherently and internally very happy about that. And as an outcome of that happiness, we get showered a lot of love from our users.

So it's not just great for emotional connect — it's also great for business. We have **12 million users** in two dialects that we are servicing until now, which is Haryanvi and Rajasthani. And out of these 12 million, about **1.2 million users are paying us a quarterly fee** to be subscribed on the platform. It doesn't end there — we are already profitable in Haryanvi and we are soon going to be profitable in Rajasthani as well.

Now it turns out that there is a massive, massive opportunity that India has to present. There are **19,000+ dialects** and STAGE as a company alone cannot even imagine covering all of them. We do have in sight roughly 20 more dialects that we want to capture. But I just want to bring this here for all the people who are trying to reach India, who are trying to listen to India at scale — that it is almost impossible to do that if we do not connect with them, if we do not reach them and meet them where they are.

[Demo of AI voice bot conversing in Haryanvi]

So you just saw this here — this is an AI bot conversing in Haryanvi. By the way, let me also tell you, **Haryanvi does not have a script. It does not have any literature whatsoever.** And that is the case with 9,000+ dialects that we have in this country. Again, a massive challenge for us to train AI.

This bot can also converse with our user in Hindi as proficiently. It detects the tonality of the user and what the user is going to speak in, and then it changes — it adapts its approach.

Another thing — one of the most powerful things that we can develop going forward is the proactivity in here. Until now, with the advent of software, we have been used to building software that is reactive in nature. The human decides to use, and the software is at its service. **But with AI we can change that paradigm.** AI can proactively reach out — as it did in this user's case. It realized that the user is facing a challenge while browsing the app and it proactively reached out to solve for that challenge.

I'll tell you something more interesting here. So when we decided to build STAGE, it was very simple for us to decide that hey, the product has to be seamlessly stitched in dialect. So we went on and we built a customer support team and we hired people who could speak Haryanvi and Rajasthani. Now just by the virtue of doing that, we saw our **refund rates fall by 50%**. That was the power of how we could emotionally connect with our user. It was not just about servicing them — it was also about emotionally connecting with them.

That being said, one more thing: on STAGE, our users range from an Olympian to a startup CEO to an IT professional to a government employee to a farmer and even a taxi driver.

There is a perception that if someone speaks in their native dialect, there's a presumption of the socioeconomic strip that they come from. Let me also tell you that our users mail us in impeccable English. So I'm here to breach that and break that perception, and I also want to tell you that this couldn't be further from the truth.

**Language is about identity and not class.** That's all. Thank you.

#### On HMT's role at STAGE (~3:00)
**Host:** "Harsh, what are you doing for STAGE? What responsibilities?"

**HMT:** "Defining responsibilities is very difficult, Madhav. As a founder, we do everything. STAGE is at a very interesting point in its journey. The things that brought us here are NOT the same things that will take us forward. All four of us have absolutely massive global ambitions. So we're laying building blocks that will become pillars for what comes next. I'm not doing anything specifically special, but if we talk skillsets — I focus on product and strategy. And 'strategy' is such a broad term that everything falls into it — new features, what they'll be... and without accountability, there's nothing in a founder's life."

#### On enjoying the journey (~4:30–10:00)
**HMT:** "Startups are a beautiful thing — no matter what tension comes, what problem comes, storms come, but the fun keeps coming continuously. We've been doing this since 2010, when the term 'startup' wasn't even popular. I remember YourStory did an article 'Daring to Start a Business in College — It's Happening!!' about when me and my erstwhile co-founder started a robotics company."

**HMT on mindset difference 2010 vs today:** "For us, it was a much more daring thing to do, frankly. Access to capital didn't exist then... We come from middle-class families, grew up in villages — all three of them literally grew up in villages, I grew up in Lucknow. 'Investor' as a concept didn't even make sense to us — we come from baniya families, if you take someone's money, it's a loan you repay with interest."

**HMT on the journey vs destination:** "In a startup, there is no destination. The journey IS it. While you're building it, you have to love it. The goal post changes every time... It's not about a final outcome. It's about enjoying every day while you're building it across the board."

#### The WittyFeed collapse (~0:00–1:15)
**Vinay:** "26 November 2018, exact date — we woke up and the company doesn't exist anymore. We had done $1M revenue that month... It's like someone healthy in your family dying in an accident vs a slow illness — accident is much harder to accept."

#### The night before the rebirth (~1:11:21)
**Vinay:** "That night [Feb 2019], we went home having decided to shut the company. Next morning, all four of us felt the same thing — we said to each other: 'This is not the climax of our movie. Our story cannot end here.'"

"Harsh was probably a little more in his senses... he didn't sleep all night, drove around, then late at night sent a long message: **'Our story doesn't end here.'**"

#### The 54 who stayed (~1:12:13)
Town hall with ~90 people. Offered: 25% salary + equity for 6 months.

**Vinay:** "To our surprise, out of 90 people, 54 said 'Sir, forget 25%, I have 1 lakh savings, 2 lakh savings — we won't let the company die.'"

"**26 February 2019 — that's the day STAGE was truly born.**"

#### On HMT's role in the crisis (~1:09:24)
**Vinay:** "Their role [Harsh's] was so important at that time — they held us, kept us standing. **We can never repay that debt.**"

#### STAGE as a revolution (~14:53–17:07)
**Parveen:** "We're not a company, we are a revolution (kranti)."

**HMT:** "Saving dialects, saving culture — these are not things corporate structures do. These require movements (andolan). Movements are built on the streets. And we're building our company in every village, every lane of Haryana and Rajasthan — not from our Noida office."

"**A people's movement is what is needed to serve, save dialects and cultures.**"

#### On reinvention (~1:16:03)
**Vinay:** "WittyFeed made text-based stories. STAGE is a full video/OTT platform — films, web series. Not a single skill from WittyFeed was relevant. Everyone reinvented themselves — article writers became scriptwriters, social media people became content heads, website coders switched to app development."

### 6.3 TiE iDay 2025 (May 2025)

**Title:** Innovating for Bharat: STAGE – India's Regional Cultures OTT | TiE's India Internet Day 2025  
**URL:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=fLwA4umIxZc  
**Duration:** 5:03 | **Format:** Fireside interview with moderator Rajesh

---

**Moderator (Rajesh):** Harsh, you are unique. You have created a digital platform which is a true representative of localization, right? You have monetized us — right now you're monetizing Bihar, right? How have you done that? It's an amazing, fascinating story — to be able to... there was an argument India is so hard to monetize in consumer, other than ad, no one paid. But you have shown that you can monetize the mofussil markets of India. So I'd love to know from you — what was your secret sauce?

**HMT:** First of all, hello everyone. Harsh on this side, one of the co-founders of STAGE. We're building an OTT for India's regional cultures.

Moving on to Rajesh's question — I don't think there was any point in the journey where we had a secret sauce. It was a constant process of figuring it out.

When we first got started, we had not set out to build an OTT. We were building a platform for local artists, because we just felt the need that there are a lot of stories which need to be told which are not told.

I remember one of the first set of feedbacks that we started getting post launching the artist-led platform was: **"Hey, thank you for saving our culture."** And we were like... [pause]

So it was actually a lot of iterative building with our users that led us to eventually figure out and crack that — hey, there are so many untold stories. There are so many stories that need a lot more voice. And it just took us in the direction where we said, hey, probably Bollywood and all of the industry put together is not doing a great job at that, and there needs to be a platform that caters to it.

**Moderator:** When we think of digital entertainment, what comes to your mind is Netflix, then maybe Spotify. But I think now we have innovation-led platforms — from STAGE to Pocket FM to Kuku to Pratilipi — which have taken shape in the last 5 years silently, and they have learned to monetize. This has been the most fascinating part of this evolution that has happened. So just take us through your ability to monetize — what is your learning there?

**HMT:** I think two major paradigm shifts played a role.

One was there was a sudden increase in willingness to pay by the audience, thanks to possibly all the shifts that happened during COVID.

Second — the fantastic government initiative called **UPI**, which has actually caused a paradigm shift in how India treats fintech and deals with fintech. And it's been such an inclusive technical infrastructure in this space.

I think both things put together — we were able to unlock the willingness to pay along with the ease of payment. And something like **autopay** effectively made it seamless for our users to just keep consuming.

Apart from that, we were very clear since the inception that we have to create value in the core content proposition. So we realized — what are the things that the users already pay for? For example, if you look at a cable TV subscription, that is something that India has been paying for for a while, right? It sort of began in the '90s, and it's not just an India-alpha phenomenon — it's a nationwide phenomenon. TV penetration goes really deep, it's about 90-95% across the country.

So that was the third insight — people are used to paying for content. It is just that the modern models and the social media age models have started monetizing eyeballs and attention over delivering core value through content.

So — social media, deeper internet penetration, UPI, local pride, local stories — combination of that: STAGE.

And we say — **we're creating something where we want every Indian to take pride in their cultural identity.**

**Moderator:** Excellent.
