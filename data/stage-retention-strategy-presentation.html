✅ proven

Content-Market Fit is Real

The 3% who watch are watching more — avg watch days up from 1.93 to 2.17. Watch frequency climbs even as the base shrinks. Uninstall rate dropped from 36% to 32.5%. Users who find content love it. The product works for engaged users.

✅ proven

TV M1 Retention is Improving at Scale

TV M1 went from 36.8% (Jul) → 42.4% (Nov) even as cohort size grew from 2K to 12K. This is the opposite of the usual pattern where quality degrades at scale. The TV experience is genuinely getting better. D30 retention of 70–82% cumulative is extraordinary.

✅ proven

Microdrama as Activation Engine

70–88% of trial users watch microdrama on D0. 15% of library drives 20% of title starts. Top shows (husband-on-sale) achieve 78% hook→complete. This is the single best top-of-funnel activation mechanism in Indian OTT.

✅ proven

Renewal Users are the Backbone

52.7K of 87K watchers (60%) are renewal users retaining at 48.4%. This base is loyal. Renewals are the bedrock — any strategy must protect and expand this segment, not just acquire new.

✅ proven

Interventions Deliver Massive M0 Lift

Pre vs post intervention: M0 watch retention went from 22.7% → 40.3% (+17.6pp) for all subscribers, and 36.2% → 50% (+13.8pp) for install+subscribe users. The structural improvements from 2025 are real.

The honest framing: STAGE built something rare — a dialect-first OTT with real content-market fit, a sophisticated CLM engine, and proven retention levers. The problem is not that nothing works. It's that the things that work reach 2–5% of the addressable base while acquisition keeps pumping the top of a leaky funnel.

10 — Resource Reallocation

What we should
stop doing

Strategy isn't just what to start. It's what to kill. Every hour spent on a low-ROI activity is an hour not spent on the three bets.

  • 🛑

    Stop spreading CLM across 1.1M dormants equally

    375K never-watched LVUs are carrier billing ghosts with 0.26% daily conversion. Targeting them wastes push notification budget and pollutes CLM performance metrics. Write off never-watched LVUs >90 days. Redirect 100% of that capacity to the 214K previously-watched HVU+MVU pool (conversion: 0.4–0.8%).

  • 🛑

    Stop commissioning microdramas over 80 minutes

    Every show above 1.5 hours has <13% completion. Kalabazari (2.3 hrs) had 57K starts and 6% completion — that's 53K users who started and abandoned. Hard cap at 4,800 seconds. No exceptions. If the story can't be told in 80 minutes, make it a series.

  • 🛑

    Stop acquiring through low-activation channels

    If 65% of M0 subs never open the app, a huge share of acquisition spend produces ghosts. Audit activation rate per channel. Any channel below 5% activation should be paused immediately. Better to acquire 50K intentional users than 163K phantoms.

  • 🛑

    Stop promoting shows below 40% hook→complete

    Shows like andheri-raat (44.6%), kiraye-ki-patni (45.8%), and especially kalabazari (23.1%) are getting recommendation slots that could go to proven titles. Every bad recommendation is a churned user. Deprioritize low-completion shows from recommendation; don't renew them.

  • 🛑

    Stop scaling Bhojpuri quantity before quality parity

    Bhojpuri has the largest subscriber base and the worst conversion (2.51% vs 3.5%). The best Bhojpuri microdrama is a Haryanvi localization — not an original. Adding more mediocre Bhojpuri titles dilutes the catalog. Fix quality first. 20 great shows > 40 mediocre ones.

  • 🛑

    Stop treating subscriber count as the primary success metric

    1.58M subscribers where 5.5% watch is a vanity metric. It drives acquisition-at-any-cost behavior. Shift the internal north star to active watchers (watched in last 30 days: ~250–300K) and weekly watch retention. Report total subs for market positioning; operate on qualified subs.

12 — Second-Order Thinking

The consequences
of doing this right

Every strategic move creates ripple effects. Here are the ones that aren't obvious.

If you cut ghost subscribers...

The subscriber count drops from 1.58M to ~300K "qualified." This changes the fundraising narrative. Investors and board members anchored on "nearly 2M subscribers" will need re-education. The trade-off is honest metrics vs. comfortable optics. Recommendation: dual reporting. Total subs for market size story, qualified subs for everything operational.

If you fix acquisition quality...

Total subscriber growth slows dramatically. The "289% YoY" headline disappears. But CAC efficiency improves (fewer wasted subs), engagement rates jump (clean denominator), and the dormant pool stops growing at 65% of inflow. Growth looks smaller but is actually real.

If you force cross-format exposure...

Users who came for microdramas will see long-format content they didn't choose. Some will bounce — short-term microdrama completion rates may dip. But the 8pp M0 retention lift is proven. Short-term engagement metrics may wobble for long-term retention gains. Communicate this to the team before shipping.

If Samsung partnership works...

TV user base could 10× in 12–18 months. But TV users consume 5× more content. Is the catalog deep enough? At scale, TV users will exhaust the content library faster, potentially accelerating the M1→M3 cliff that already exists. TV distribution without content velocity = a more expensive version of the same problem.

14 — The Uncomfortable Questions

What we need to answer
honestly

These are the questions that don't have comfortable answers. They should be discussed, not deferred.

Question 01
Are we building a business on 87K watchers and calling it 1.58M?
Only 5.5% of subscribers actively watch. If a SaaS company had 5.5% DAU/MAU, investors would call it a zombie product. OTT is different — not everyone watches daily — but the 13:1 dormant ratio means STAGE's subscriber number is more of an acquisition metric than an engagement metric. At what point does the gap between reported subscribers and actual users become a credibility risk with the board, with investors, with ourselves?
Question 02
Is the M1 #3 ranking masking a content depth crisis?
STAGE retains at 53% in M1 (remarkable) but drops to 22% by M3 — the steepest cliff in the peer set. Netflix drops 13pp over the same period. JioHotstar drops 12pp. STAGE drops 31pp. The interpretation matters: if it's content exhaustion (users run out of things to watch), STAGE needs 3–5× more content velocity. If it's novelty decay (the app isn't sticky enough independent of content), it's a product problem. These require very different investments. Which is it?
Question 03
Can STAGE survive on a dialect-first content strategy long-term?
Haryanvi dominates every metric: best watch conversion, best hook→complete, 64% of TV WAU. Bhojpuri — the largest subscriber base — has the worst conversion and its best show is a Haryanvi localization. Rajasthani and Gujarati are too early. Is "dialect OTT" actually "Haryanvi OTT that happens to have other dialects"? If the content-market fit is fundamentally stronger in Haryanvi, should resources concentrate there instead of spreading thin?
Question 04
What if carrier billing subs are the business model, not the product?
Here's the uncomfortable reframe: if 65% of M0 subs come through carrier billing and they never watch, STAGE is effectively monetizing telco distribution — not content. The revenue from ghost subscribers may subsidize the product for the 87K who do watch. Cutting ghosts is the right thing for metrics and decisions, but what's the revenue impact? Does the unit economics story still work at 300K qualified subscribers?
Question 05
Is STAGE's competitive moat content or distribution?
The board deck positions STAGE as #3 in M1 retention — a content quality signal. But the dormant analysis suggests the real scale comes from distribution deals (carrier billing, Samsung). If a well-funded competitor (Chaupal, JioCinema) decides to invest in dialect content, does STAGE's content advantage hold? Or is the real moat the distribution relationships that produce the 1.58M number — the very same channels that create the ghost problem?
Question 06
Are we measuring improvement or survivorship bias?
WoW retention "recovered" from 40.8% to 44.5% in the last week. But the watcher base also shrank from 94K to 87K. When casual users churn, the remaining base is more engaged — the percentage goes up because the denominator got more selective. The Feb 16 recovery may not be a recovery at all. It might be the metric getting more flattering as the base gets smaller. How do we tell the difference?
Question 07
If we fix everything in this deck, is the TAM big enough?
Assume success: 150K active watchers, 48% WoW retention, quality acquisition. That's a real business serving dialect-language entertainment. But is it a venture-scale business? Netflix India has ~30M subscribers. JioHotstar has ~50M. The dialect market may be structurally smaller. At some point, STAGE either needs to expand beyond dialects or accept a different growth trajectory. When does that conversation happen?
15 — The Bottom Line

STAGE built something real. The structural wins from 2025 — CLM, microdramas, TV, long-format series — are proven and intact.

The problem isn't finding what works. It's that the things that work reach 2–5% of the base while acquisition keeps pumping the top of a leaky funnel. The next 90 days aren't about new ideas. They're about scale and focus.
