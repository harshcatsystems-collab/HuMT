Summary
Parveen Singhal led the team in aligning on fundamental business realities, emphasizing that content is the core reality influencing acquisition, reacquisition, and re-engagement, and urging a shift to an LTV-centric viewpoint. The discussion established that the three key business outcomes linked to content are acquiring, re-engaging, and reacquiring users, with Trial Conversion Rate (TCR) and Subscription Rate (SR) being controllable through content and promotion strategies. Key metrics for content performance include Watch Rate (WTR) and Completion Rate (CR), and the team agreed to focus efforts on projects that directly impact core metrics, such as addressing the 1.1 million dormant subscribers, while preparing for a pan-India expansion.

Details
Setting the Agenda and Achieving Alignment: Parveen Singhal initiated the meeting by stating the objective: to align the team on the fundamental business realities that anchor the organization, even as the means to achieve those goals may change. The goal of the discussion is to collaborate, gather perspective, and ensure fundamental alignment so that all teams, and areas their work influences, can move forward together efficiently. They emphasized that the team should be united, operating with a teaming skill set, without compromising on the quality of their thinking.
Content Strategy and Business Outcomes: The discussion focused on how content—including its format, topics, and titles—impacts acquisition, reacquisition, and re-engagement. Parveen Singhal explained that different content formats solve different problems, noting that feature films (or single-serve content) tend to be "dead content" and yield a lower Trial Conversion Rate (TCR) compared to binge-worthy content. The content strategy should be based on identifying content intent drivers and structuring content acquisition accordingly, with the understanding that content is the core reality.
Influencing Trial Conversion Rate (TCR) and Re-engagement: It was suggested that the TCR and Subscription Rate (SR) can be positively influenced, even before a user starts a trial journey, by creating intent through promotional content. Additionally, the potential for reacquisition and re-engagement differs across users, necessitating a differentiated approach to performance advertising for each group. The discussion also touched upon other solutions, including pricing and direct subscriptions, which will be discussed later, but the immediate focus was on onboarding the team onto these fundamental ways of thinking.
Shifting to an LTV-Centric Viewpoint: A key point for the team is to start evaluating everything from a Lifetime Value (LTV) perspective. This shift involves recognizing that the initial acquisition experience, such as a feature film, can confuse a user, emphasizing the need for differentiated reacquisition or re-engagement strategies. A fundamental shift in thinking is required, moving away from treating all content pieces as equal and recognizing that "Each content is a unique product".
Identifying Three Key Business Outcomes Linked to Content: The fundamental thinking has shifted from viewing the business through a "content factory" lens where all acquisition is equal, to a model that identifies at least three distinct business outcomes directly linked to content: acquire, re-engage, and reacquire. While content may fill a specific bucket from a fulfillment standpoint, the discoverability of that content must be uniform. It was noted that this approach, validated by experiments, represents the correct pathway forward.
Controlling Metrics via Content and Promotion: Parveen Singhal discussed the importance of understanding which funnel or metric each piece of content is intended to solve before sanctioning it. They explained that promotion can significantly control metrics like TCR, citing experiments that showed, for example, promoting short-form content (8-minute episodes) as part of a family watch experience could increase the TCR. The team is aligned on leveraging validation and insights from experiments to build user confidence and intent.
Defining the Acquisition Funnel: The team affirmed that acquisition must be viewed as including activation. It was established that the funnel consists of acquisition, activation, reacquisition, and re-engagement, with the acknowledgment that activation, including the "aha moment" and "habit moment," is critical for LTV. The objective is to drive business outcomes, and projects are merely the means to achieve those ends, which remains constant regardless of technological shifts like AI.
Value Creation and Business Metrics: The focus must be on value creation for the user, which is reflected in business metrics. It was stressed that everything must be solved together and not in silos to avoid recurring problems. The content is the main product, feeding both growth and retention, and the success of the platform depends on the health of its core metrics.
Measuring Content Performance: Watch Rate and Completion Rate: Two main metrics matter for content: Watch Rate (WTR) and Completion Rate (CR). WTR is an internal distribution metric reflecting how many users in a subscription cohort start watching content. CR is defined as the metric of content quality and depth, serving as a quality marker for content. Frequency, which is essential for user retention, is preemptively influenced by content strategy.
Focusing on Growth Metrics and Retention: Time is identified as the highest lever for driving growth, which can be increased by expanding markets, content types, or formats. For the trial model, key metrics include Trial Rate (TR), D0 TCR, and D1 retention. If the growth journey is managed effectively up to the subscription rate, it leads to positive Customer Acquisition Cost (CAC) and Lifetime Value (LTV) outcomes.
Influencing Renewal Rate and Defining Cohorts: The most aggressive metric for retention is the subscription percentage, defining the cohort based on the "habit moment" (eight active watch days in the first month). Analysis showed that the biggest factor influencing renewal rate is the user's recency to the renewal date, not achieving the "habit moment" early in the journey. The team uses M1 plus watch percentage to track people who have stuck with the platform, defined as users who watched in the last 28 days and form the most active cohort.
Focusing on Dormant Subscribers and Core Business Outcomes: SPEAKER_1 identifies that the 1.1 million dormant subscribers, who have previously been paying, are the "biggest pain in the ass for sale right now". They state that they have not been able to focus on these dormant watchers because they were too focused on other areas, and solving this is the biggest pain point. The speaker emphasizes that acquisition, reacquisition, and re-engagement are the core business outcomes that capture the entirety of value creation, and all efforts, including AI-native and AI-first projects, need to be directed toward these goals.
Prioritizing Projects Based on Core Metric Impact: They advise team members to prioritize efforts that directly impact the core metrics, using the example of a trailer creation tool versus character words. The acquisition strategy should be prioritized since the total market is a "bigger tank," but reacquisition strategy can be market-to-market deferred. SPEAKER_1 noted that D1 retention is comparatively much weaker than D3 retention, but that "do watch percentage" among dormant users is a very bad sign and the "biggest red flag".
Addressing Retention and Platform Quality: Retention is identified as a very important metric related to the quality of the platform, not just the lifetime value (LTV). The real value is already 65–67% optimized, suggesting further optimization should focus on factors other than 'watch retention' itself. SPEAKER_1 mentioned an upcoming RBI link that will monitor app installs or uninstalls, which may actually work in their favor, while confirming the moral and business necessity of chasing month-on-month revenue as a key metric.
Defining Content Metrics and Frequency: SPEAKER_1 explains that content creation should aim to maximize frequency, which is completion by the user. They clarify that "potential frequency" cannot be a business metric because it is not an outcome of business activity, but rather a creative output of content strategy, preferring metrics like "Active watch days". They conclude that for content, two objective measurement metrics are distribution (WTR) and content quality (CR), but CR alone is not sufficient for longer-form content.
Standardizing Content Performance Metrics: The challenge of standardizing metrics for content is acknowledged as an open-ended question, though frequency must be part of content evaluation. Active days created by content and watch timelessness are proposed as quality and depth metrics, respectively, to measure how much depth a content piece creates in a user's watch cycle. They summarize that the goal of a metric is to cause alignment in improving the most core fundamental things of the business, and they will use an iterative process rather than chasing a perfect metric immediately.
Expansion and Cultural Relevance: SPEAKER_1 shares that the company will become a pan-India company within the next 45 days, enabled by a solution now in place, which will lead to future brainstorming on this expansion. They discuss the importance of the cultural wrapper, which acts as a decision maker for users by making the platform part of their consideration set, though content will only work if the core filmmaking quality is good. They mention that going deep into different dialects like Rajasthani or Gujarati might make the company unique, but acknowledge the risk of being looked at as a "language dialect first company" versus a culture-first company.
Immediate Next Steps and Team Structure: SPEAKER_1 mentions that 38% TCR (Subscription) is a more important signal than the conversion numbers, which are okay. The next steps involve allocating people across all the defined matrices who will own them and moving forward aggressively. SPEAKER_1 confirms that the planning involves three hypotheses: every team member is a full-stack builder, one person at the senior level owns one metric, and these small teams will be created to tackle property statements and ensure accountability.
