# Pelvic Floor Workshop: Understanding & Care During Pregnancy

**Facilitator:** Sheetal Anand (Pre & Postnatal Expert)  
**Duration:** ~58 minutes  
**Audience:** Pregnant women across all trimesters

---

## Executive Summary

This workshop covers the anatomy, function, and care of the pelvic floor during pregnancy and postpartum. Key insight: **The pelvic floor needs to be dynamic and flexible, not just tight.** Training should adapt by trimester — supportive in early pregnancy, prepared for loosening near labor.

---

## 1. What is the Pelvic Floor?

### Anatomy

**Pelvic Floor Bones (Pelvic Girdle)**
- Bowl-shaped structure covering from the sides
- The hard part you feel at hip level where you wear your lowers
- Includes the sit bones (what you sit on)

**Pelvic Floor Muscles**
- Group of muscles and connective tissues forming a **hammock-like structure** at the base of the pelvis
- Attaches from pelvic bone to tailbone, and from one sit bone to the other
- Acts as the foundation supporting all abdominal organs: liver, kidney, intestines, uterus, vagina, bladder

### Key Visualization
> **Think of a hammock:** When you sit on it, it sinks down. When you get up, it relaxes back. That's how your pelvic floor works — it needs to be flexible enough to support weight AND return to neutral.

## 2. Functions of the Pelvic Floor

| Function | Description |
|----------|-------------|
| **Organ Support** | Supports bladder, uterus, and rectum |
| **Bowel & Bladder Control** | Controls urination, bowel movements, and gas |
| **Spinal Stabilization** | Attached to tailbone; helps stabilize spine and pelvis |
| **Sexual Function** | Plays a role in sexual health |
| **Works with Diaphragm** | Coordinates with breathing for core pressure management |

## 3. The Breathing Connection (Critical Insight)

### How Breathing Moves the Pelvic Floor

**On Inhale:**
- Diaphragm muscle moves DOWN
- Pelvic floor also moves DOWN (like someone lying on the hammock — it sinks)

**On Exhale:**
- Diaphragm moves UP
- Organs move IN
- Pelvic floor comes BACK UP and tightens

### Diaphragmatic Breathing Practice
1. Sit straight, relax spine
2. Place hands on sides of chest (rib area, not waist)
3. Inhale deeply — feel expansion from sides, front, and back (like a rubber ring expanding)
4. Exhale — feel everything return

> **This breathing reaches your pelvis.** The breath creates movement that trains pelvic floor flexibility naturally.

## 4. Why Pelvic Floor Matters During Pregnancy

### The Core Problem
- Uterus sits directly on pelvic floor muscles
- As baby grows, weight on pelvic floor increases dramatically
- Untrained pelvic floor = complications during pregnancy, labor, and recovery

### What a Healthy Pelvic Floor Needs
**NOT just tight. NOT just loose. DYNAMIC.**

| Quality | Why It Matters |
|---------|---------------|
| **Flexible** | Must stretch to support increasing weight |
| **Strong** | Must hold organs in place |
| **Controlled** | Must respond to your intentional tightening/loosening |
| **In Rhythm** | Must coordinate with breath and movement |

### The Hammock Analogy — Problems at Both Extremes

**If Too Tight:**
- Hammock material is rigid, non-stretchable
- Weight causes tearing
- Baby can't descend during labor
- Increases risk of tearing during delivery

**If Too Weak:**
- Hammock sinks all the way down
- Organs lose support
- Incontinence (loss of bladder control)
- Heaviness/dragging sensation

## 5. Trimester-Specific Pelvic Floor Care

### First Trimester
**Goal:** Build support as weight begins increasing

- Focus on STRENGTHENING exercises
- Kegels are appropriate here
- Want pelvic floor to hold firm

### Second Trimester  
**Goal:** Maintain support, prevent premature descent

- **DON'T** hold deep squats (malasana) for long durations
- **DON'T** over-loosen — you don't want baby descending too early
- Moderate Kegels okay (not aggressive)
- Dynamic movements preferred over static holds

### Third Trimester (35+ weeks)
**Goal:** Prepare for baby's descent and labor

- **REDUCE** tightening exercises
- **INCREASE** loosening/relaxation work
- Baby needs to descend — tight pelvic floor resists this
- Squats, hip openers, relaxation become primary
- Decrease Kegel frequency significantly

## 6. The Kegels Myth — IMPORTANT

### What Kegels Are
- Tightening and releasing pelvic floor muscles
- Same feeling as holding your pee and letting go

### The Problem
**Kegels are NOT always beneficial:**

| Situation | Should You Do Kegels? |
|-----------|----------------------|
| First trimester | ✅ Yes, helpful for building support |
| Second trimester | ✅ Yes, but moderate frequency (once daily or weekly) |
| Third trimester (35+ weeks) | ⚠️ Reduce significantly — you need LOOSENING, not tightening |
| Pelvic floor already too tight | ❌ No — will worsen problems |
| Near labor | ❌ No — need to push, not hold |

### Common Mistake
> Women hear "do Kegels" and do them constantly from first trimester until delivery. This can cause the pelvic floor to be TOO TIGHT when it needs to LOOSEN for labor.

### Kegel Risks When Overdone
- Pain during labor
- Difficulty pushing
- Increased risk of tearing

## 7. Signs of a Weak Pelvic Floor

| Symptom | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| **Urinary Incontinence** | Unable to control/hold pee (most common) |
| **Heaviness/Pressure** | Dragging feeling in pelvic area |
| **Difficulty Walking/Standing** | Simple movements become hard |
| **Low Back/Hip Pain** | Excessive pain (some is normal, severe is a sign) |
| **Constipation** | Gut/bowel issues despite good diet |
| **Difficulty Controlling Bowels** | Gas or bowel incontinence |
| **Pelvic/Pubic Pain** | Discomfort in the area |
| **Vaginal Bulge** | Extreme case — seek immediate help |

## 8. How to Care for Your Pelvic Floor

### Daily Practices

| Practice | How It Helps |
|----------|--------------|
| **Diaphragmatic Breathing** | Naturally trains pelvic floor flexibility through breath |
| **Good Posture** | Reduces unnecessary strain |
| **Soft Seating Surface** | Use cushions on hard chairs |
| **Relaxation** | Releases tension; nervous system signals muscles to loosen |
| **Jaw Loosening** | Relaxing jaw signals whole body (including pelvic floor) to relax |
| **Perineal Massage** | Gentle massage over pelvic area to improve blood circulation |
| **Prenatal Yoga** | Guided practice for full-body mobility including pelvic floor |

### What NOT to Do

| Don't | Why |
|-------|-----|
| Constantly suck belly in | Creates chronic tightness |
| Aggressive Kegels all day | Overtightens pelvic floor |
| Hold urine frequently | Strains muscles |
| Ignore heaviness or leaks | Early signs of problems |
| Overdo squats without awareness | Can harm without proper guidance |

## 9. Postpartum Pelvic Floor Care

### What Happens During Delivery
- Natural delivery completely loosens pelvic floor (especially during pushing)
- Control over muscles is temporarily lost
- The "hammock" is still there but very stretched

### Recovery Approach

**DO:**
- Start with **gentle breathing** — natural breath helps strength return gradually
- Be patient — flexibility returns naturally with proper care
- Begin postnatal yoga when cleared by doctor
- Watch for warning signs

**DON'T:**
- Rush into core exercises
- Do heavy lifting early
- Ignore persistent issues

### Warning: Diastasis Recti
- Overdoing core work postpartum can cause separation of abdominal muscles
- Creates "mummy tummy"
- Gentle, guided practice is essential

---

## 10. When to Seek Professional Help

Consult a doctor immediately if you experience:

| Red Flag | Description |
|----------|-------------|
| **Persistent Leakage** | Incontinence that doesn't improve |
| **Severe Pelvic Pain** | Pain that restricts daily movement |
| **Feeling of "Falling"** | Sensation of something dropping/falling down |
| **Painful Recovery** | Postpartum recovery with significant pain |
| **Visible Bulge** | Any bulging in vaginal area |

> **Early help = faster recovery.** Don't wait to address these symptoms.

## 11. Key Exercises by Trimester

### First & Second Trimester
- Kegels (moderate)
- Cat-cow (with awareness)
- Hip rotations
- Standing movements

### Third Trimester (Labor Prep)
- Malasana (supported squats) — held longer
- Standing side lunges
- Elevated lunges
- Kandasana
- Dharyasana
- Hip opening movements

### Postpartum (3-6 months post, with doctor approval)
- Gentle breathing
- Gradual core activation
- Postnatal yoga

## 12. Quick Reference Summary

### The Core Message
> **Pelvic floor = hammock supporting your organs. Keep it flexible, controlled, and in rhythm with your breath.**

### Key Principles

1. **Not just tightening** — train for dynamic flexibility
2. **Breath is your best tool** — during pregnancy, labor, AND recovery
3. **Strength + Relaxation = Balance**
4. **Care now = smooth recovery later**
5. **Listen to your body** — awareness is everything

### The Hammock Rule
- Too loose → sinks, organs fall, incontinence
- Too tight → may tear, can't push during labor
- **Just right** → flexible, controlled, responds to your needs

## Q&A Highlights

**Q: Can Kegels cause cervix to open too early?**  
A: No, if practiced under professional guidance with trimester-appropriate intensity.

**Q: How does jaw loosening help pelvic floor?**  
A: When you loosen your jaw, your nervous system signals your WHOLE body to relax, including pelvic muscles.

**Q: What about pelvic pain at night?**  
A: Try gentle hip rotations before sleep to activate blood circulation.

**Q: Birthing ball benefits?**  
A: Provides softer surface than hard chairs, reducing pressure on pelvic floor.

**Q: Can women with C-section do pelvic floor exercises?**  
A: Generally after 3-6 months, with doctor's approval.
