Part 1: What I Learned (The Most Important Section)

The Journey: Three Passes, Two Corrections

Feb 23: I shipped v1 with 65% ghost rate, concluded "primarily an acquisition quality problem," and moved on.

Feb 27: Nikhil Nair (Growth Pod Lead) asked a simple question in our MPDM: "Can you show M0 data excluding web trials?"

What happened next exposed my first fundamental error.

Learning #1: I Used the Wrong Segment Definition

What I did wrong: I tried to identify "web trials" using acquisition source fields (media source, campaign name). Found only 1.3% of users, concluded web trials were negligible.

What Nikhil taught me: Web trials are identified by the PLATFORM field on fct_user_subscription_history (values: app/web/tv), not by ad attribution.

The correction: Web trials = 25% of M0 base (45.7K users), not 1.3%. When removed, ghost rate drops from 46% → 34%. Web trials are the ghost problem.

What this revealed about my thinking: I approached the problem top-down from attribution systems instead of bottom-up from user behavior. I assumed "web" meant "came from web ads" when it actually meant "subscribed via web platform." I conflated how they found us with how they subscribed.

Nikhil's implicit critique: "Don't dismiss small segments just because they're low volume — make sure you're measuring the right thing first."

Mar 2: I posted the corrected analysis (v2) to #engagement-solver-team. Within hours, Vismit Bansal (Retention Lead) responded with 5 explicit requirements:

  1. Marketing channel-level breakdown
  2. High-intent vs low-intent segmentation
  3. Distinguish Acquisition Quality vs Marketing Push (temporal analysis)
  4. Use Watcher% + Habit% as north stars (not watch conversion)
  5. Installed cohort + direct subscription analysis

Learning #2: I Was Measuring the Wrong Metrics Entirely

❌ What I Focused On (v1)

  • Watch conversion (3.09%)
  • Activity buckets (0, 1, 2-4, 5-7, 8+ days)
  • Dialect-level aggregates
  • Uninstall rate

These are funnel metrics — they measure conversion at a point in time. They don't distinguish between types of users or quality of acquisition.

✅ What I Should Focus On (v3)

  • Watcher% (% who watch at all)
  • Habit% (% who watch 7+ days)
  • Channel-level quality
  • Temporal trends (is quality improving or degrading?)

These are cohort health metrics — they measure the quality of the user base and whether we're acquiring the right people.

Why this matters: Watch conversion (v1's focus) treats all M0 subscribers as the same. A carrier-billing ghost and an organic app install both count as "M0 subs" in the denominator. This masks channel quality differences.

Watcher% by channel (v3's focus) reveals that Facebook Ads drives 54.8% watchers while organic drives 33.5%. That 21pp difference = the entire M0 problem. You can't see it in aggregated watch conversion.

Vismit's explicit critique: "You're using the wrong north star. Stop measuring funnel conversion. Start measuring cohort quality."

Where Nikhil and Vismit's Feedback Overlaps

At first glance, they seem like different critiques:

  • Nikhil: Technical correction about segment definitions
  • Vismit: Strategic redirect about metrics and segmentation

But they're saying the same thing:

Stop looking at aggregates. Segment ruthlessly. Different user cohorts behave completely differently — averaging them together produces meaningless numbers.
  • Nikhil caught me treating "web trials" and "app trials" as one cohort when they have 2x different ghost rates
  • Vismit caught me treating "Facebook Ads" and "organic" as one cohort when they have 1.6x different Watcher%

The unified insight: STAGE doesn't have one M0 engagement problem. It has 15 different problems across 15 acquisition channels, masked by aggregate reporting.

What This Teaches Me About Product Analytics

Five Principles I'll Carry Forward

1. Start with the data model, not the intuition. I assumed "web trials" meant web ads. I should have started by asking "what fields does STAGE actually track?" Data models are ground truth. Intuition is often wrong.

2. Challenge the metric before optimizing it. I spent v1 analyzing watch conversion deeply without asking "is this the right metric?" Vismit's question — "why not Watcher% + Habit%?" — forced me to rethink the entire framing.

3. Small segments can reveal big insights. I dismissed web trials as "only 1.3%" and moved on. Nikhil didn't. He knew they were larger and asked me to dig deeper. Never dismiss a segment because it looks small — make sure you're measuring it correctly first.

4. Aggregate metrics hide more than they reveal. The 65% ghost rate (v1) felt like one problem. When segmented by channel (v3), it's 15 different problems with wildly different causes. Organic is 66.5% ghost. Facebook Ads is 45.2% ghost. These require different solutions.

5. Temporal analysis catches degradation early. Vismit asked for "Acquisition Quality vs Marketing Push" — essentially, is ghost rate trending up or down over time? I hadn't checked. When I did (v3), I found ghost rate worsening (60% → 76% over 4 months). That's a crisis, not a steady-state problem.

4. Platform Segmentation: App vs Web vs TV (Nikhil's Correction)

Nikhil's insight: Web trials behave fundamentally differently than app trials. Don't lump them together.

Web trials are the ghost rate driver. They represent 25% of M0 volume but contribute 43% of all ghosts. Removing web trials would drop blended ghost rate from 66.1% → 34.2% (Nikhil was exactly right).

Why web trials fail:

  • No app install barrier — easier to sign up on impulse
  • Desktop/mobile web UX likely worse than app
  • May not even own the device where they subscribed (shared computer)
  • Trial flow may lack app install nudge

Recommendation: Either (1) kill web trials entirely, or (2) change web trial flow to require app install within 24 hours to maintain trial status. Current web trial economics are destroying M0 metrics.

Part 3: The Unified Insight — What It All Means

The Three Passes, Synthesized

v1 (Feb 23): "65% ghost rate. This is an acquisition quality problem."
What I got right: Identified acquisition quality as a lever.
What I got wrong: Treated all M0 as one cohort. Used wrong metrics (watch conversion instead of Watcher%). Missed platform segmentation entirely.

v2 (Mar 2, post-Nikhil): "Web trials = 25% of M0, 80.5% ghost. Removing web drops ghost rate to 34%."
What I got right: Corrected platform segmentation. Isolated web trials as primary ghost driver.
What I got wrong: Still using funnel metrics. Still missing channel-level breakdown. No temporal analysis.

v3 (Mar 2, post-Vismit): "STAGE has 15 different M0 problems across 15 channels, masked by aggregate reporting. Paid acquisition works (50%+ Watcher%). Organic is broken (33.5% Watcher%). Ghost rate worsening over time (60% → 76%). Web trials are economic poison. App-installed cohort is healthy (57.7% Watcher%)."
What this reveals: The full picture. The acquisition quality problem has three distinct causes: (1) web trial flow design, (2) organic acquisition mix (too heavy on carrier billing), (3) recent degradation (Jan/Feb 2026 volume spike from low-quality sources). Each requires a different solution.

Part 4: What to Do About It (Definitive Recommendations)

P0 Kill or Fix Web Trials (Impact: -23pp ghost rate)

Current state: Web trials = 25% of M0 volume, 80.5% ghost rate, 0.9% Habit%. They destroy blended metrics and produce almost zero long-term value.

Option A (aggressive): Kill web trial flow entirely. App-only acquisition.
Option B (surgical): Require app install within 24 hours of web signup to maintain trial. Auto-cancel if not installed.
Option C (test & iterate): A/B test web trial flow changes (e.g., "Install app to start watching" CTA on web signup confirmation).

Owner: Growth Pod (Nikhil) + Product (Pranay)
Timeline: 2 weeks to decide, 1 week to implement
Success: Web trial % drops from 25% → <10% of M0 volume, or web trial Watcher% improves from 19.5% → 40%+

P0 Audit Jan/Feb Acquisition Changes (Impact: Stop the bleeding)

Crisis: Ghost rate jumped from 63.7% (Dec) → 68.1% (Jan) → 75.4% (Feb). Something specific changed in late Dec / early Jan that flooded M0 with ghosts.

Action: Pull all acquisition channel changes, marketing campaigns, partnership launches, and trial flow updates between Dec 15, 2025 and Jan 31, 2026. Correlate with volume spikes.

Likely culprits:

  • Carrier billing partnership scaled without quality gates
  • Display ads campaign with poor targeting (googleadwords ACI_Display shows 61.9% ghost rate)
  • Referral program gaming (if organic spiked)
  • Trial flow simplified too much (lower friction = lower intent)

Owner: HMT + Nikhil (Growth) + Vismit (Retention)
Timeline: This week. Urgent.
Success: Identify root cause and kill/pause it. Ghost rate stabilizes by end of March.

P1 Scale Paid Acquisition (Impact: +4.6pp Watcher%)

What works: Paid ads (Facebook, Instagram, Google) drive 50.3% Watcher% vs 33.6% organic. But they're only 1.7% of volume.

Opportunity: Scale paid from 1.7% → 10% of M0 acquisition. At current quality (50% Watcher%), this lifts blended Watcher% from 33.9% → 38.5%.

Constraint: LTV/CAC. Need to validate paid channels are profitable at scale.

Experiment: 2x paid spend for 30 days (from ~10K/month → 20K/month). Track cohort Watcher%, Habit%, and M1 conversion. If LTV/CAC holds, scale to 50K/month.

Owner: Growth Pod (Nikhil) + Finance (budget approval)

Success: Paid acquisition scales to 5-10% of M0 volume with LTV/CAC ≥ 3.0

Closing Reflection: What I'll Do Differently Next Time

Five Commitments

1. Validate segment definitions before analyzing. Don't assume "web trials" = web ads. Check the schema. Ask the person who built the data model.

2. Challenge the metric in the brief. Before diving into analysis, ask: "Is this the right metric? What would Vismit ask me to measure instead?"

3. Segment first, aggregate never. Every analysis starts with "what are the natural segments?" (platform, channel, time, dialect, intent). Aggregates are for executive summaries only, never for insights.

4. Include temporal trends by default. "What does this look like over the last 6 months?" is a mandatory question. Steady-state problems and degradation crises require different responses.

5. Pressure-test my own conclusions. Before shipping, ask: "What would Nikhil's correction be? What would Vismit's 5 requirements be? What am I missing?" Invite the critique preemptively.

The meta-lesson: Good analytics isn't about running queries. It's about asking the right questions, measuring the right things, and segmenting ruthlessly. v1 failed because I didn't do any of those. v3 works because I learned from Nikhil and Vismit. The numbers are the same. The thinking is completely different.
