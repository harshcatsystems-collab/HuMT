1. Meeting Summary

This was a pivotal internal strategy meeting at STAGE where the content team presented and debated a fundamental organizational restructuring: shifting from a culture-based team model (where each regional culture — Haryanvi, Rajasthani, Bhojpuri — independently managed all content formats) to a format-based pod model (where teams are organized around content formats — Long Series, Binge Series, Feature Film, Micro Drama — with cultural expertise embedded within each pod). This builds on a decision made ~8 months ago to shift from "Culture First" to "Entertainment First."

The meeting then pivoted to a deep, sometimes heated debate about content formats and their strategic purpose — specifically whether "binge series" (limited series, 5-10 episodes released at once or in batches) deserves to be a distinct format, or whether all episodic content should be unified under "long series." The discussion revealed a tension between the business imperative for frequency (getting users to return daily/weekly, which drives retention and renewal) and the consumer need for variety (different mental models for consuming different types of stories). Long series with episodic weekly release was identified as the primary mechanism for driving frequency and habit-building.

The meeting also covered the current content pipeline (135 active projects, ₹48 Cr total budget, 72% still in pre-shoot phases), the role of AI in scriptwriting (as a framework/analysis tool, NOT as a story generator), dubbing economics being transformed by AI, and the strategic importance of creating universal "STAGE IPs" rather than culture-specific IPs. Budget reallocation discussions touched on reducing feature film spend and doubling down on long series.

3. Key Topics Discussed

A. Organizational Restructuring: Culture-Led → Format-Led Pods

The Problem with Culture-Led Model:

  • Knowledge silos: breakthroughs in one culture (e.g., Haryanvi) never reached others (e.g., Bhojpuri)
  • No standardized SOPs across cultures
  • Unhealthy competition between culture teams
  • Inconsistent output quality
  • Dubbing treated as "orphan child"
  • Doesn't scale — adding Punjabi, Bengali, etc. means spinning up entire new teams

The New Format-Led Pod Structure:

  • 4 Pods: Long Series, Binge Series, Feature Film, Micro Drama
  • Each pod has format expertise + embedded cultural expertise
  • One SOP per format, applied across all cultures
  • New culture = plug into existing pods (no rebuild from scratch)
  • Dubbing gets format ownership — no longer an afterthought

Pod Leadership:

  • Long Series → Renu (format + Rajasthani expertise)
  • Binge Series → Nidhi (G5/Rangbaaz expertise)
  • Feature Film → Parvesh (+ temporary Micro Drama ownership)
  • Micro Drama → TBD (hiring needed)

Production team (Sushant) stays centralized but aligns meetings by format, not culture.

B. Format Strategy & Purpose

Each format serves a distinct user purpose:

  • Long Series → Habit builder (drives daily/weekly frequency)
  • Binge Series → Completion driver / seasonal habit builder
  • Feature Film → Depth solver (90-120 min sweet spot; completion drops beyond 120 min)
  • Micro Drama → Gap filler (travel/commute consumption)

The Big Debate — Is Binge Series a Separate Format?

Vinay challenged whether binge series deserves separate format status, arguing:

  • Mathematically, all episodic content is the same — just different slice sizes of a long story
  • Business needs frequency → long series (episodic, weekly release) is the answer
  • What consumer problem does binge solve that long series + feature film can't?

Counter-arguments (Parveen, Nidhi, others):

  • "The cake is different, not just sliced differently" — the architecture/framework of a limited series is fundamentally different
  • Different genres demand different formats (crime may not work in ultra-long series)
  • User mental model matters: limited series = commitment-friendly (I know it ends in 6-8 episodes)
  • Variety in the content bouquet matters for platform perception and willingness to pay
  • Brand value and IP creation work differently in binge format

Resolution: No final decision. Agreed to continue the debate with more data. Release strategy (binge vs episodic vs personalized) identified as a separate problem from format definition.

D. AI in Content Creation

  • Script Analyzer tool being developed — uses storytelling frameworks (Save the Cat, anti-hero frameworks, etc.) to strengthen scripts
  • Key principle: "Story human se nikalni hai, story AI se nahi nikalni hai" (Stories must come from humans, not AI). AI is for framework-driven technical strengthening only.
  • AI asking probing questions about story elements (murder mystery example: "Why is the door locked? From inside or outside? Who discovered it?") — impressive early results
  • AI dubbing economics transforming: ~₹8,000/episode dubbing cost could enable cultural productization of any content across all markets
  • Vision: If originals model cracks, AI model follows easily — "50 GPUs lagake" (just throw GPUs at it)

E. Key Metrics & Business Model Shifts

  • Primary metric: Completion rate (center metric)
  • New release is NOT dependent on performance — performance titles run independently, new releases are add-ons
  • Economy of scale shift: Instead of ₹5 Cr → 10 films seen by few, now ₹5 Cr → 5 films but maximize reach to 1 lakh+ viewers each
  • Frequency = unique active days of user; Depth = watch time + completion rate
  • Cultural wrapper concept: Content has a universal core (story, cast, quality, genre, theme). Culture is a "marketing wrapper" — like Neem on toothpaste. Makes it more relatable/consumable, but the base product must be strong first.

F. Release Strategy Discussion

  • Netflix binge model being questioned industry-wide
  • Hotstar, Amazon shifting toward episodic release
  • Personalized release floated as future possibility: different users get different release cadences based on their behavior
  • Maha Punar Janam example: users who joined late displayed binge behavior anyway — all content eventually becomes binge-able in catalog

6. Strategic Insights

What This Reveals About STAGE's Content Strategy Direction

  1. STAGE is betting its future on long-running episodic content — the "daily soap" model adapted for OTT. This directly addresses their core business challenge: getting users to return frequently enough to justify monthly subscriptions. This is a significant pivot from the earlier diverse-format approach.
  1. The "Entertainment First" shift is real and deep — 8 months in, they're now restructuring the entire organization around it. Culture is being repositioned from a creative driver to a "marketing wrapper." This is philosophically significant: STAGE was founded on regional culture, and they're now explicitly subordinating it to entertainment quality.
  1. Format consolidation is coming — Mini Film is dead, Micro Drama is in limbo (no expert, parked on Parvesh), and Binge Series is being questioned at the CEO level. The trajectory points toward a simplified model: Long Series (primary) + Feature Film (secondary) + everything else TBD.
  1. AI is being positioned as infrastructure, not content creator — The script analyzer approach is smart: use AI frameworks to strengthen human stories, not replace human creativity. The dubbing cost revolution (₹8K/episode) could be transformative for their cultural productization strategy.
  1. The ₹48 Cr pipeline is partially stranded — 72% of projects are pre-shoot under the OLD strategy. Significant organizational energy will go toward triaging these: which to continue, convert, or kill. Sunk cost decisions ahead.
  1. Universal IPs over cultural IPs — The shift from "Haryanvi IP" and "Rajasthani IP" to "STAGE IP" that can be culturally wrapped for any market is a scale-enabling decision. This also de-risks individual culture failures.
  1. Competitive positioning vs Cuckoo FF — While not explicitly discussed in this meeting, the long series strategy directly competes with Cuckoo FF's micro-drama model (they're doing ₹150 Cr/month on China-licensed short content). STAGE is betting on the opposite: depth over breadth, originals over licensed.
