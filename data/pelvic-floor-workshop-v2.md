# The Complete Guide to Pelvic Floor Care During Pregnancy

**Based on workshop by Sheetal Anand, Pre & Postnatal Expert**

---

## At a Glance

> **The #1 Insight:** Your pelvic floor isn't just about being "strong" — it needs to be **flexible, controlled, and dynamic**. Like a hammock that can hold weight AND spring back.

**The Kegels Truth:** They're helpful in early pregnancy, but doing them constantly until delivery can make labor HARDER. Your pelvic floor needs to loosen for baby's descent.

---

## Part 1: Understanding Your Pelvic Floor

### What It Is

Your pelvic floor is a group of muscles shaped like a **hammock** at the base of your pelvis. It:

- Stretches from your pubic bone to your tailbone
- Connects your two sit bones
- Supports your bladder, uterus, and rectum
- Controls when you pee, poop, and pass gas

### The Hammock Visualization

Imagine lying in a hammock:
- **When you lie down** → it sinks to support your weight
- **When you get up** → it springs back to neutral

Your pelvic floor does the same thing — it must be able to BOTH stretch under pressure AND return to its resting state.

### Why It Matters in Pregnancy

Your uterus sits directly on this hammock. As baby grows:
- Weight on pelvic floor increases dramatically
- Muscles must adapt to support this changing load
- Untrained muscles → problems during pregnancy, labor, AND recovery

## Part 2: The Breathing Connection

**This is the insight most women miss.**

Your breath directly moves your pelvic floor:

| When You... | Diaphragm... | Pelvic Floor... |
|-------------|--------------|-----------------|
| **Inhale** | Moves DOWN | Stretches DOWN (like someone lying in the hammock) |
| **Exhale** | Moves UP | Returns UP (like hammock springing back) |

### Why This Matters

Every deep breath is a mini pelvic floor exercise. Diaphragmatic breathing naturally trains flexibility — no Kegels required.

### How to Practice

1. Sit straight, shoulders relaxed
2. Place hands on sides of ribs (not waist)
3. Inhale slowly — feel ribs expand sideways like a rubber ring
4. Exhale — feel everything return
5. Repeat for 2-3 minutes daily

## Part 3: Trimester-by-Trimester Guide

### First Trimester: BUILD SUPPORT

**Goal:** Strengthen the hammock as weight starts increasing

✅ **Do:**
- Kegels (tighten-hold-release pelvic muscles)
- Core engagement exercises
- Diaphragmatic breathing

❌ **Avoid:**
- Nothing specific to avoid yet

---

### Second Trimester: MAINTAIN & PROTECT

**Goal:** Keep pelvic floor supportive — don't let baby descend too early

✅ **Do:**
- Moderate Kegels (once daily or a few times weekly)
- Dynamic movements (not static holds)
- Continue breathing practice

❌ **Avoid:**
- Holding deep squats (malasana) for long periods
- Aggressive daily Kegels
- Over-loosening exercises

---

### Third Trimester (35+ weeks): PREPARE TO RELEASE

**Goal:** Get pelvic floor ready to LOOSEN for labor

✅ **Do:**
- Hip-opening exercises
- Supported squats (malasana) — now held longer
- Side lunges, elevated lunges
- Relaxation and jaw loosening
- Perineal massage

❌ **Avoid:**
- Frequent Kegels (you need loosening, not tightening!)
- Exercises focused on "holding" or "tightening"

## Part 4: The Kegels Myth — CRITICAL READING

### What Everyone Gets Wrong

Most women hear "do Kegels" and do them constantly from first trimester until delivery day.

**This is a mistake.**

### The Truth About Kegels

| Situation | Kegels? | Why |
|-----------|---------|-----|
| First trimester | ✅ Yes | Building support as weight increases |
| Second trimester | ✅ Moderate | Maintaining without overdoing |
| 35+ weeks | ⚠️ Reduce | You need to LOOSEN for baby's descent |
| Already tight pelvic floor | ❌ No | Makes the problem worse |
| During labor | ❌ No | You need to PUSH, not hold |

### What Happens If You Overdo Kegels

- Pelvic floor becomes too tight
- Baby can't descend easily
- Pushing during labor becomes harder
- **Increased risk of tearing**

### The Right Approach

Think of Kegels as ONE tool, not THE tool. Balance with:
- Breathing exercises
- Hip openers
- Relaxation
- Massage

## Part 5: Warning Signs

### Signs Your Pelvic Floor Is Too Weak

| Symptom | What It Means |
|---------|---------------|
| Can't hold pee (incontinence) | Loss of muscle control |
| Heaviness or dragging feeling | Organs not fully supported |
| Severe low back/hip pain | Spinal stabilization affected |
| Constipation (despite good diet) | Bowel control affected |
| Difficulty with simple movements | Muscles not supporting daily activities |

### When to See a Professional IMMEDIATELY

🚨 **Don't wait if you experience:**

- Persistent leakage that doesn't improve
- Severe pelvic pain restricting daily movement
- Sensation of something "falling" inside
- Visible bulge in vaginal area
- Painful postpartum recovery

**Early help = faster recovery.**

## Part 6: Daily Care Practices

### The Essentials

| Practice | How Often | Why It Helps |
|----------|-----------|--------------|
| Diaphragmatic breathing | 2-3 min daily | Natural pelvic floor training through breath |
| Good posture | Always | Reduces unnecessary strain |
| Soft seating | When sitting long | Hard surfaces = extra pressure |
| Jaw relaxation | Throughout day | Relaxed jaw signals body to release tension everywhere |
| Gentle movement | Daily | Keeps blood flowing, muscles responsive |

### What NOT to Do

❌ Constantly sucking belly in (creates chronic tightness)
❌ Aggressive Kegels all day
❌ Holding urine frequently
❌ Ignoring warning signs
❌ Intense squats without professional guidance

## Part 7: Postpartum Recovery

### What Happens During Delivery

- Natural delivery stretches pelvic floor significantly
- Muscles are temporarily very loose
- Control needs to be rebuilt gradually

### Recovery Approach

**Weeks 1-6:**
- Focus ONLY on gentle breathing
- Let natural recovery happen
- Don't rush into exercise

**After 6 weeks (with doctor clearance):**
- Begin gentle core activation
- Start postnatal yoga
- Gradually rebuild strength

**3-6 months (for C-section):**
- Core and pelvic floor work begins later
- Always get doctor's approval first

### Warning: Don't Rush Core Exercises

Overdoing core work postpartum can cause **diastasis recti** — separation of abdominal muscles that creates a persistent belly bulge.

## Part 8: Quick Reference Card

### The 3 States of Pelvic Floor

| State | What It Means | The Problem |
|-------|---------------|-------------|
| **Too Tight** | Hammock pulled taut, no give | May tear; blocks baby's descent |
| **Too Loose** | Hammock sags to ground | Organs unsupported; incontinence |
| **Just Right** | Flexible, controlled, responsive | Can tighten when needed, loosen when needed |

### The Core Principles

1. **Dynamic > Static** — Train for flexibility, not just strength
2. **Breath is everything** — Natural breathing trains pelvic floor automatically  
3. **Trimester matters** — What helps at 12 weeks may hurt at 38 weeks
4. **Listen to your body** — Awareness is your best tool
5. **Recovery is part of the journey** — Care now = easier postpartum

### All Trimesters
- Diaphragmatic breathing
- Cat-cow (with awareness)
- Gentle hip rotations
- Pelvic tilts

### Third Trimester Additions
- Malasana (supported squats) — held
- Standing side lunges
- Elevated lunges
- Kandasana
- Hip openers

### Postpartum (After Clearance)
- Gentle breathing first
- Gradual core activation
- Guided postnatal yoga

## The Bottom Line

Your pelvic floor is the foundation supporting your entire pregnancy journey. Treat it like the dynamic, responsive system it is — not just a muscle to "tighten."

**The goal isn't strength. It's CONTROL.**

When you can consciously tighten AND consciously release your pelvic floor, in rhythm with your breath — you're ready for labor, delivery, and recovery.
